# Screenprinting at Home

A beginner's guide to pulling your first prints without a studio.

## What You'll Need

### Essential Equipment

- **Screen** --- aluminium frame with 110T mesh (A4 or A3)
- **Squeegee** --- slightly narrower than your screen's inside width
- **Photo emulsion** --- diazo-based (e.g. Speedball or Jacquard)
- **Ink** --- water-based textile ink for fabric, acrylic for paper
- **Transparency film** --- for printing your design (inkjet, maximum black)
- **Work light or sunlight** --- for exposing the screen
- **Spray bottle** --- for washing out the design

### Nice to Have

- Hinge clamps (for registering multi-colour prints)
- Heat gun or iron (for curing textile ink)
- Scoop coater (for even emulsion coating)
- A dark room or closet (for coating screens)

## Preparing Your Design

### Creating the Artwork

Your design needs to be **high contrast black on clear**. The black areas block light during exposure; everything else washes away to become your stencil.

Good sources for designs:

1. Hand-drawn with thick markers, scanned at 600dpi
2. Vector art exported as solid black
3. High-contrast photographs converted to halftone
4. Photocopied collages

### Printing the Transparency

Print your design onto transparency film using an inkjet printer set to maximum ink density. Hold it up to a light --- if you can see through the black areas, print a second copy and stack them for denser coverage.

## Coating the Screen

### Setting Up

Work in a dimly lit room. Photo emulsion is light-sensitive once mixed, so avoid direct sunlight or bright overhead lights.

1. Mix the diazo sensitizer into the emulsion per the manufacturer's instructions
2. Pour a bead of emulsion along the scoop coater
3. Hold the screen vertically
4. Pull the coater up the **print side** (flat side) in one smooth motion
5. Flip and do one pass on the **squeegee side** (recessed side)
6. Let it dry horizontally, print-side down, in complete darkness

### Drying

Emulsion must be **bone dry** before exposure. Use a fan in a dark room, or a hair dryer on cool. This takes 1--2 hours depending on humidity. The screen should feel smooth and slightly tacky.

## Exposing the Screen

### The Setup

1. Place your transparency **face-down** on the **print side** of the screen (reading backwards when you look at it)
2. Weight it down with a sheet of glass to ensure full contact
3. Place a black surface behind the screen

### Exposure Time

Exposure time varies by light source:

| Light Source | Distance | Time |
|-------------|----------|------|
| 500W halogen | 30cm | 8--12 min |
| UV lamp (20W) | 15cm | 4--6 min |
| Direct sunlight | --- | 2--4 min |
| Overcast daylight | --- | 8--15 min |

**Test strips are essential.** Coat a screen, cut it into strips, and expose each for different durations. The shortest time that gives a solid stencil is your target.

### Washing Out

Immediately after exposure, spray both sides of the screen with a gentle shower of water. The unexposed emulsion (under your black design) will soften and wash away, leaving your stencil. Hold it up to a light to check for pinholes.

## Printing

### Setup

1. Tape the screen to your work surface or use hinge clamps
2. Place your substrate (paper, fabric, etc.) underneath
3. Pour a bead of ink above your design area

### The Pull

1. Hold the squeegee at 45 degrees
2. Pull firmly toward you in one smooth stroke
3. The ink passes through the open mesh onto your substrate
4. Lift the screen carefully

### Tips for Clean Prints

- **Flood stroke first** --- push ink gently across the design without pressing down, then pull with pressure. This pre-fills the mesh.
- **Consistent pressure** --- uneven pressure gives uneven prints
- **Don't over-pull** --- one firm stroke is better than three light ones
- **Snap-off distance** --- keep a 2--3mm gap between screen and substrate so the mesh peels away cleanly behind the squeegee

## Curing and Finishing

### Paper Prints

Water-based ink on paper air-dries in 15--30 minutes. No further treatment needed.

### Textile Prints

Textile ink needs heat-setting to survive washing:

1. Let the print air-dry completely (24 hours is safest)
2. Iron on the reverse side for 3--5 minutes at the fabric's maximum temperature
3. Or use a heat press at 150°C for 30 seconds
4. Or tumble dry on high for 30 minutes

### Test

After curing, wash a test print. If the ink survives a machine wash without fading, your cure is good.

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Blurry edges | Screen not flat on substrate | Check snap-off distance |
| Pinholes in print | Pinholes in stencil | Fill with emulsion, re-expose |
| Ink bleeding | Too much ink | Lighter squeegee pressure |
| Stencil washing away | Underexposed | Increase exposure time |
| Stencil won't wash out | Overexposed | Decrease exposure time |
| Ink drying in screen | Printing too slowly | Work faster, mist screen |

## Multi-Colour Printing

### Registration

To print multiple colours, you need to align each layer precisely. The simplest method:

1. Print your first colour
2. Mark the substrate position with tape on your work surface
3. Switch to the next screen (same hinge clamp position)
4. Print the second colour

For tight registration (less than 1mm tolerance), use **registration marks** --- small crosshairs printed on each transparency that align across layers.

### Colour Order

Print **lightest colours first**, darkest last. White before yellow before red before blue before black. Transparent inks blend where they overlap --- this can be a feature or a problem depending on your design.

### Drying Between Colours

Each layer must be touch-dry before printing the next. Water-based inks: 10--20 minutes. Speed up with a heat gun on low.

## Cleanup

1. Scrape excess ink back into the container
2. Rinse the screen immediately with water (before ink dries)
3. For stubborn ink, use a soft brush
4. To reclaim the screen for a new design, use emulsion remover

Water-based inks clean up with water. This is one of their best features.
