## Common Edible Plants

### Dandelion (*Taraxacum officinale*)

Every part is edible. Young leaves for salads (before flowering --- less bitter). Flowers for fritters or wine. Roots roasted as a coffee substitute.

**Where:** Lawns, parks, cracks in pavements. Everywhere.

**When:** Leaves best in early spring. Flowers in late spring.

/column

### Stinging Nettle (*Urtica dioica*)

Rich in iron and vitamins. Cook or dry them --- heat destroys the sting. Excellent in soup, pasta, or as a tea.

**Where:** Woodland edges, riverbanks, neglected corners.

**When:** Young shoots in spring (top 4--6 leaves). Avoid after flowering.

**Caution:** Wear gloves when picking.

### Wild Garlic (*Allium ursinum*)

Unmistakable garlic smell when you crush a leaf. Makes incredible pesto. Flowers are edible too.

**Where:** Damp woodland, shaded river paths.

**When:** March to May. Look for carpets of broad green leaves.

**Caution:** Leaves resemble lily-of-the-valley (toxic). Always crush and sniff --- garlic smell confirms it.

/column

### Elderflower (*Sambucus nigra*)

Flat clusters of tiny white flowers with a heady, sweet scent. Use for cordial, champagne, or battered fritters.

**Where:** Hedgerows, woodland edges, railway embankments.

**When:** Late May to early July.

**Tip:** Pick on a sunny day when the pollen is dry. Don't wash --- you'll rinse off the flavour.

### Blackberry (*Rubus fruticosus*)

The gateway foraging plant. Everyone knows what a blackberry looks like. Best eaten warm from the bramble.

**Where:** Hedgerows, railway lines, wasteland, anywhere neglected.

**When:** August to October. Stop after the first frost (old folklore says the devil spits on them).

/column

### Rosehip (*Rosa canina*)

The fruit of wild roses. Extremely high in vitamin C. Best as a syrup or tea --- too many seeds and fine hairs to eat raw.

**Where:** Hedgerows, scrubland, field edges.

**When:** September to November, after the first frost softens them.

**Processing:** Split, de-seed, then simmer. Strain through muslin to remove irritating hairs.

### Hawthorn (*Crataegus monogyna*)

Young spring leaves known as "bread and cheese" --- mild, nutty flavour, good in sandwiches and salads. Berries (haws) make excellent ketchup and jelly.

**Where:** Hedgerows, woodland edges, old field boundaries.

**When:** Leaves in April--May. Berries in September--October.

**Tip:** Haw ketchup is surprisingly good with cheese. Simmer berries, sieve out stones, add vinegar and spices.

/column

### Sweet Chestnut (*Castanea sativa*)

Not to be confused with horse chestnut (conkers). Sweet chestnuts have spiny, hedge-like husks and pointed nuts. Roast over a fire or boil and puree.

**Where:** Parks, old estates, mixed woodland. Common in southern England and across Europe.

**When:** October to November. Gather from the ground after they fall.

**Caution:** Always score the shell before roasting or they'll explode.

### Chamomile (*Matricaria chamomilla*)

Daisy-like flowers with a distinctive apple scent when crushed. Dry the flower heads for the best herbal tea you'll ever have --- leagues ahead of teabag versions.

**Where:** Field edges, disturbed ground, sandy soils. Often grows on paths.

**When:** June to August. Pick when fully open on a dry day.

**Caution:** Can be confused with mayweed (scentless, sometimes irritant). Crush and sniff --- real chamomile smells sweet and apple-like.

/column

### Fat Hen (*Chenopodium album*)

One of the oldest food plants in Europe, pre-dating agriculture. Use like spinach --- the young leaves are mild, slightly mealy, and high in protein for a green.

**Where:** Gardens, allotments, field edges, anywhere with disturbed soil.

**When:** June to September. Pick young leaves and shoot tips.

**Tip:** Cook briefly like spinach. Also good raw in salads when very young.
